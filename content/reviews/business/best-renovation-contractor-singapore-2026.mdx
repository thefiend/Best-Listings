## What to Look for When Hiring a Renovation Contractor in Singapore

**HDB eligibility and licensing.** Not every renovation contractor is approved to work on HDB flats. HDB requires its Renovation Contractor Scheme (RCS) registration for firms carrying out structural or regulated works. Confirm that any contractor you hire holds the appropriate HDB RCS licence before signing a contract. Unlicensed HDB renovation, particularly structural hacking, can result in fines and mandatory reinstatement costs.

**Itemised quotations.** A professional renovation contractor in Singapore provides a written, itemised quotation that breaks down cost by work category: demolition, tiling, carpentry, electrical, plumbing, and painting. Quotations that lump all costs into a single line or describe scope vaguely are a red flag. Itemised quotes are the only way to compare proposals accurately and to hold contractors accountable if scope disputes arise mid-project.

**Contract clarity and payment schedule.** The renovation contract should specify scope, materials (brand and grade), timeline milestones, and a progress payment schedule. Never pay more than 20% as an initial deposit. For HDB projects, CASE recommends a maximum of 10% upfront. Progress payments should be tied to completed work stages, not arbitrary dates.

**Review volume and recency.** A 4.5-star rating with 200 recent reviews is far more meaningful than a perfect score from 8 reviews. Check whether the reviews span the past 12 months and cover a range of project types. Sudden clusters of 5-star reviews posted within days of each other are a common pattern of review manipulation.

**Defects liability period.** Reputable renovation contractors provide a defects liability period (typically 12 months) covering workmanship defects that appear after handover. Get this in writing and confirm what it covers. Defects arising from materials supplied by the client are typically excluded, but defects from contractor workmanship should be rectified at no additional cost.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much does a renovation contractor cost in Singapore?
Full HDB 4-room renovation ranges from SGD 30,000 to SGD 60,000 for standard finishes. Premium finishes and extensive carpentry can push this to SGD 80,000 or more. Condo renovations average SGD 40,000 to SGD 100,000 depending on size and scope. Landed property full renovation starts at SGD 80,000 and scales significantly with the extent of structural or M&E works involved.

### How do I choose a renovation contractor in Singapore?
Check HDB RCS registration if your project involves an HDB flat. Request itemised written quotations from at least three contractors. Verify Google reviews are recent and voluminous. Ask for references from completed projects. Confirm the payment schedule ties to milestones, not dates, and ensure a defects liability period is included in the contract.

### What is a typical renovation timeline in Singapore?
A standard 4-room HDB renovation takes 6 to 10 weeks from start of works to handover. Condo renovations are typically 8 to 12 weeks. Landed property renovations with structural works can run 3 to 6 months. Factor in an additional 2 to 4 weeks for permit applications if structural works or window replacements are involved.

### Do renovation contractors in Singapore need a licence?
For HDB flats, contractors carrying out regulated renovation works (structural hacking, window replacement, waterproofing) must be registered under the HDB Renovation Contractor Scheme. For electrical work, the contractor or their appointed electrician must be licensed by SP Group and the Energy Market Authority. General painting and minor carpentry do not require licensing, but HDB structural works do.

### What should be included in a renovation contract in Singapore?
The contract must include: scope of works with specific materials listed, project start and completion dates, milestone-based payment schedule, contractor's HDB RCS number (for HDB projects), defects liability period, and process for handling variation orders. Contracts missing any of these elements leave homeowners exposed to disputes.

### How far in advance should I book a renovation contractor?
For HDB BTO renovations, book 3 to 4 months before your key collection date. For resale HDB or condo, allow 2 to 3 months between signing a contract and starting works. Reputable contractors fill their schedules quickly, especially from January to March and June to August, which are peak renovation months in Singapore.

### What are red flags when hiring a renovation contractor in Singapore?
Red flags include: refusing to provide itemised written quotations, requesting more than 20% as an upfront deposit, no verifiable physical address or business registration, pressure to sign within 24 hours without time to compare, unusually low quotations that exclude standard scope items, and contractors who cannot confirm HDB RCS registration when asked.

### What is the difference between a renovation contractor and an interior designer in Singapore?
A renovation contractor executes physical works: tiling, carpentry, painting, electrical, plumbing. An interior designer provides space planning, design concept, and material specification, and typically engages a contractor to execute the build. A design-and-build firm does both under one contract. If you have a clear design in mind, a direct renovation contractor is typically 15 to 25% cheaper than a full-service interior design firm.

### Can I renovate my HDB flat without a contractor?
Minor works, painting, installing furniture, and replacing fixtures are allowed as DIY. Structural hacking, wet works in toilets and kitchens, window replacement, and electrical panel changes require licensed contractors under HDB regulations. Attempting regulated HDB renovation work without a licensed contractor can result in fines and mandatory reinstatement.

### What is a defects liability period for renovation in Singapore?
A defects liability period (DLP) is a contractual guarantee period, typically 12 months, during which the contractor rectifies workmanship defects at no charge. Defects include cracked tiles, uneven finishes, leaking pipes, or faulty carpentry joints appearing after handover. DLPs should be written into the contract and clearly state which defects are covered and the rectification response time.
