## What to Look for When Hiring Divorce Lawyers in Singapore

**Specialisation in family law.** Divorce law in Singapore is a specialist discipline that intersects the Women's Charter, the Administration of Muslim Law Act, the Children and Young Persons Act, and CPF and HDB regulations. Engage a firm whose primary practice area is family law, not a general practitioner who handles divorce cases alongside commercial or property work.

**Mediation credentials and approach.** Singapore's Family Justice Courts require mediation before contested proceedings proceed. Choose a lawyer who is genuinely committed to mediated outcomes where appropriate — not one who frames every matter as litigation-ready from the first consultation. A lawyer willing to pursue a negotiated settlement will typically achieve better outcomes faster and at lower cost.

**Transparent fee structure.** Family law fees in Singapore can escalate significantly in contested matters. Insist on a written fee estimate and ask your lawyer to distinguish between fees for uncontested versus contested scenarios. Lawyers who cannot give you a realistic cost range at the outset may not have your best interests in mind.

**Communication and emotional sensitivity.** Divorce is invariably stressful, and the quality of your lawyer's communication — how they explain complex legal issues, how quickly they respond to queries, and how they support you emotionally as well as legally — significantly affects your experience of the process. Look for practitioners who listen carefully, explain clearly, and treat you as an individual rather than a case number.

**Track record in your specific situation.** A lawyer with extensive experience in uncontested divorces may not be best placed for a contested custody battle, and vice versa. Ask specifically about the lawyer's experience with matters similar to yours — the assets involved, the custody considerations, and any international or cross-border dimensions.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does a divorce take in Singapore?

An uncontested divorce typically takes three to six months from the date the divorce papers are filed to the final judgement. A contested divorce — where the parties disagree on the divorce itself or on ancillary matters such as asset division or custody — typically takes one to two years, and complex matters can take longer. The compulsory mediation process at the Family Justice Courts adds time but generally reduces total duration for cases that settle at mediation.

### How much does a divorce lawyer cost in Singapore?

For an uncontested divorce, legal fees typically range from SGD 1,500 to SGD 3,000 depending on the complexity of the ancillary matters. A contested divorce can cost significantly more — fees of SGD 15,000 to SGD 50,000 are common for fully litigated matters, and complex cases involving significant assets or protracted custody disputes can exceed SGD 100,000 in total legal costs. Many firms offer fixed fees for simple uncontested divorces.

### What are the grounds for divorce in Singapore?

Under the Women's Charter, the sole ground for divorce in Singapore is the irretrievable breakdown of the marriage, which must be established through one of five facts: adultery, unreasonable behaviour, desertion for at least two years, separation for three years with the other party's consent, or separation for four years without the other party's consent. The parties must also have been married for at least three years, unless exceptional hardship or depravity is demonstrated.

### What is the difference between an uncontested and contested divorce?

In an uncontested divorce, both parties agree on the divorce itself and on all ancillary matters including asset division, child custody, and maintenance before court proceedings commence. In a contested divorce, the parties disagree on one or more of these issues, requiring the court to adjudicate after hearing evidence from both sides. Uncontested divorces are significantly faster and cheaper than contested proceedings.

### What happens to the HDB flat in a divorce?

HDB flat ownership in divorce is governed by both Singapore family law and HDB eligibility rules. The court considers the flat as part of the matrimonial asset pool and can order various outcomes: transfer to one party (subject to HDB eligibility), division and sale with proceeds shared between the parties, or deferred sale until children reach adulthood. CPF refund obligations apply on any sale. It is important to understand HDB eligibility requirements before agreeing to any arrangement involving the flat.

### How is child custody determined in Singapore?

Singapore courts apply the welfare of the child as the paramount consideration in all custody and access decisions. Joint custody (both parents sharing legal responsibility) is now common, with care and control (the child living primarily with one parent) granted to one parent and reasonable access to the other. The court considers factors including the child's age, the quality of each parent's relationship with the child, the parent's ability to provide a stable environment, and the child's own wishes if old enough to express them.

### Can I get a divorce without a lawyer in Singapore?

Technically yes — Singapore law does not require legal representation in divorce proceedings. However, the procedural complexity of even uncontested divorces, the need to properly document ancillary agreements in consent orders, and the significant financial and custody implications of errors make self-represented divorce inadvisable for most individuals. Legal aid may be available through the Legal Aid Bureau for those who qualify on means grounds.

### What is the Muslim divorce process in Singapore?

Muslim divorces in Singapore are handled by the Syariah Court under the Administration of Muslim Law Act, not the Family Justice Courts. Available divorce types include talak (husband-initiated), fasakh (wife-initiated on specific grounds), and khuluk (divorce by mutual consent in exchange for consideration). Ancillary matters such as mahr, maintenance (nafkah iddah), and custody (hadhanah) are determined under Islamic family law principles. HDB and other civil assets may involve parallel civil court proceedings.
