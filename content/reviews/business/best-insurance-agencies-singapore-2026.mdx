## What to Look for When Choosing an Insurance Provider in Singapore

**MAS licensing and financial strength.** All life and general insurers operating in Singapore must be licensed by MAS and maintain minimum Risk-Based Capital (RBC2) ratios. Check that your insurer's financial strength rating from S&P, Moody's, or AM Best is investment grade. Financial strength matters most for long-term life policies where you are relying on the insurer's ability to pay claims 20–40 years from now.

**Claims settlement experience.** Singapore's insurance regulator publishes dispute statistics, but the most direct signal is client reviews that describe actual claims experiences rather than purchase satisfaction. Reviews mentioning specific claim types, turnaround times, and settlement outcomes are far more informative than generic ratings. Beware of insurers with high star ratings from purchase reviews but visible clusters of claims complaints on platforms like SingSaver or MoneySmart forums.

**Policy exclusions and limitations.** No two policies are the same. Common variations include: pre-existing condition exclusions (critical illness and health), suicide exclusions (life policies, typically waived after two years), adventure sport exclusions (personal accident), war and terrorism exclusions (travel), and waiting periods (disability income, typically 60–90 days deferred period). An advisor who does not walk you through the exclusions before you buy is not doing their job.

**Product match to life stage.** Insurance needs change significantly from your 20s through to retirement. A young professional needs term life, hospitalisation, and critical illness; a family breadwinner needs higher life coverage and disability income; a retiree needs primarily health coverage and potentially an annuity. An advisor who recommends a complex investment-linked product to a 25-year-old who needs straightforward protection is prioritising commission over your interests.

**Advisor credentials and independence.** Financial advisory representatives in Singapore must hold the CMFAS Module 5 and Module 9 qualifications as a minimum. Check that your advisor is registered on the MAS Financial Institutions Directory. Tied agents (who represent only one insurer) can only recommend their employer's products. Independent financial advisors (IFAs) can recommend across insurers. For complex needs, an IFA comparison adds value that a tied agent structurally cannot provide.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What insurance is mandatory in Singapore?

For HDB flat owners with an outstanding bank loan: fire insurance is mandatory, purchased through an HDB-approved insurer. For all mortgage borrowers: Mortgage Reducing Term Assurance (MRTA) is strongly recommended, though not legally mandatory. For all Singapore employees: MediShield Life basic health coverage is compulsory and automatically included in CPF deductions. For vehicle owners: at minimum third-party liability motor insurance is required to legally drive on Singapore roads.

### What is an integrated shield plan and do I need one?

An Integrated Shield Plan (IP) is a private insurance policy that supplements your basic MediShield Life coverage, extending hospitalisation coverage to higher ward classes (Class A/B1 or private hospital), paying for costs above MediShield Life limits, and sometimes including additional riders for pre- and post-hospitalisation outpatient treatment. IPs are offered by seven approved insurers in Singapore, including Prudential, AIA, Great Eastern, NTUC Income, Singlife, Raffles Health Insurance, and AXA. Whether you need one depends on your preference for ward class, your risk tolerance for out-of-pocket medical costs, and your ability to pay premiums that increase with age.

### How much life insurance do I need in Singapore?

A standard rule of thumb is 10–15 times your annual income in death and total permanent disability coverage. For a Singapore household with two income earners, each should carry coverage approximately equal to their individual income contribution multiplied by the years remaining until their youngest child is financially independent. Critical illness coverage should ideally be at least 3–5 times annual income, as the purpose is income replacement during treatment and recovery rather than just covering medical bills.

### What is CPFIS and can I use CPF for insurance premiums?

The CPF Investment Scheme (CPFIS) allows CPF OA and SA funds to be invested in certain investment-linked insurance products (ILPs). However, regular insurance premium payment from CPF is generally limited to specific endowment and whole life products that are CPFIS-included. Most term life, health, and general insurance premiums must be paid in cash. Consult your CPF statement and financial advisor before assuming your CPF OA can cover ongoing premium commitments.

### What happens to my Singapore insurance policies if I emigrate?

Policies remain valid as long as premiums are paid. However, some health insurance policies (particularly integrated shield plans tied to Singapore's MediShield Life) may not be useful if you are receiving medical treatment overseas. Term life and critical illness policies typically remain in force regardless of your country of residence. Disability income policies may have residency conditions. Review policy terms and inform your insurer if your country of residence changes permanently.

### What is the difference between term life and whole life insurance?

Term life provides a death benefit for a specified period (e.g., 20 years or until age 65/70) at a lower premium. There is no cash value — it is pure protection. Whole life provides coverage for the entire lifetime and accumulates a cash value component that grows over time and can be partially surrendered or borrowed against. Term life is more cost-efficient for pure income replacement during working years; whole life is preferred for legacy planning and situations where permanent coverage is important. Most Singapore financial advisors recommend a combination of both.

### How do I check if my insurer is MAS-licensed?

Visit the MAS Financial Institutions Directory at mas.gov.sg and search under "Insurers." All licensed life and general insurers operating in Singapore are listed with their licence type, registered address, and contact details. Purchasing insurance from an unlicensed entity provides no regulatory protection and policyholders have no recourse through the Financial Industry Disputes Resolution Centre (FIDReC).
